## Unied Risk Management (URM) Overview

Unified Risk Management (URM) is a holistic team approach to identifying, validating, and mitigating risks across all aspects of the organization in an integrated manner that aligns with the  Unified Engagement Model (UEM) framework.

<!-- image -->

The Value Realization pillar of the Unified Engagement Model (UEM) furthers its impact by implementing Unified Risk Management (URM). This evolution marks an important shift in identifying and mitigating risk, allowing for broader transparency and earlier risk engagement with our customers. URM represents a shift from a single-owner model to encompass the entire Account Team. The Unified Risk Management process, like the Success Planning process, starts on day one after closing the deal.

Why the change to URM?

As our roles and systems evolved, updating our approach to Risk Management was necessary.  We needed to :

- Align the Risk Management process to the UEM methodology
- Engage the full Account Team in the Risk Management Process to improve our assessment and management of risk
- Reduce process fatigue by introducing new system-based support for the Risk Management Process
- Have a central location as the source of truth for the Account team and the Splunk Village to document, view, and manage risk activities

Every account or opportunity could have a risk that might jeopardize a sale, renewal, upgrade and/or expansion. Everyone on the account team should know and understand the importance of risk, help identify and classify it, build and execute mitigation plans, and have a regular cadence around it as they manage your accounts. We must all consider risk to protect annual recurring revenue (ARR), while seeking expansion/upgrade opportunities . That starts with you to ensure our customers are happy!

You should have a holistic relationship and view of the account to look for potential churn, ARR down-sell risks, and insight into future renewals and long-term risks (a full year ahead).

Everyone should think of risk, regardless of their role on the account team.

## What is a risk?

You should start thinking about risk directly after the deal closes, such as what could delay implementation or block adoption, which could derail future expansion/upgrade opportunities and create a poor customer experience or negative customer sentiment. Understanding risk early allows you to be proactive instead of reactive, which is often too late. Although an RSM, CSE, or SE has ultimate accountability based on inputs and perspectives from the village, they can bring forward numerous Splunk resources to help address and mitigate each risk type. The entire account team is responsible for working together as a unified team to mitigate risks.

There are two types of risk: business and technical. Flip each card to learn more.

## Technical Risk

A Business Risk refers to the potential uncertainties and adverse events that can impact Splunk's ability to attract, retain, and serve its customers. A Regional Sales Manager (RSM) or Customer Success Executive (CSE) (if available) on the account team manages business risks.

A Technical Risk refers to the potential uncertainties and adverse scenarios that impact the ongoing Splunk product implementation and adoption needs of the customer. A Solutions Engineer (SE) on the account team manages technical risk .

## Success Plans Overview

Success Plans , a Salesforce Value Plan component, are also part of the Value Realization pillar of UEM. Like the URM process, the Success Planning process starts on day 1 after the deal closes to ensure an eective delivery of Splunk's products and services to meet your customer's objectives and needs. You should continually update the plan, including adding elements from your risk reviews, mitigation plans, and other inputs from your customer reviews,  Value Platform Assessments (PVP, SSF, etc.), Health Checks, Technical Adoption Plan, etc.

- There are two versions of Success Plans:
- MVP version of the Success Plan includes business outcomes and objectives
- Required for ALL accounts
- Inputs directly from Value Stack / Customer
- Complete Success Plan version includes business outcomes, objectives, and tasks/activity steps for each objective with owners and due dates recorded and kept current in SFDC.
- Required for accounts with more than $1 million ARR or more than $500k in pipeline
- The RSM is accountable for the orchestration of the Success Plan where there is no CSE assigned
- Technical sellers provide technical adoption milestones for the Success Plan
- Establishing a baseline of account's people (EDU), process (KPIs and dashboards), technology (data sources and integrations)
- Other inputs from the UEM Team may include Splunk Success Framework (SSF) from Tech Success Engineers, Health Check from Tech Success Engineers, Prescriptive Value Path (PVP) from Solutions Engineers (SE), or Statement of Work (SOW) from Professional Services (PS), etc.

More information about Success Plans can be found on the SRC UEM Team Seller Page

.

## Why Use Success Plans?

Success Plans are necessary for every customer because:

- They provide a mechanism to document in SFDC as you build lasting relationships with your customers, understand their ever-evolving needs, and stay engaged throughout their journey with continuous lifecycle selling .
- They directly impact our bottom line, proactively guiding our customers to see the value in our products/services and making them more likely to stick with us with increased renewals &amp; customer retention .
- They provide a continuous feedback loop from our customers with regular calls. This feedback allows us to adapt, refine our approach, and tailor our oerings to meet the customer's ever-changing needs.
- We become proactive issue solvers by anticipating and addressing potential challenges before they become major roadblocks (risks), enhancing customer satisfaction, and demonstrating our commitment to their success by providing proactive resolution .
- They also provide a distinct competitive advantage with a holistic solution that supports the customer's vision and journey.

## How do Success Plans relate to Risks?

Risk mitigation and Success Plans are separate yet connected. URM allows a risk CTA to be associated with a Success Plan. Success Plans and risk management plans are information that feeds into the Customer Business Review (CBR), another mechanism to help customers realize value with Splunk. As you build out your customer Success Plans, it is essential to consider the risks blocking the customer's objectives and the tasks to complete them. Success Plans allow you to associate appropriate CTAs (Calls to Action) and Risks in Salesforce so the entire account team has visibility and can address them as a team. Sales and CS leaders use Success Plans as a critical way to plan for executive customer engagement.

## What is a RACI Chart?

RACI stands for R esponsible, A ccountable, C onsulted, and I nformed. It is a responsibility assignment matrix that defines team roles across the four categories. It helps clarify who does the work, who makes the decisions, whose options matter, and who needs to be aware of what's happening throughout the process.

## RACI Denitions

| Lane        | Definition                                                                                                    |
|-------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Responsible | Owns and does the work to complete the task                                                                   |
| Accountable | Delegates work and are the last ones to review the task or deliverable before it's deemed complete            |
| Consulted   | Provides input based on how it will impact their project work or their domain of expertise on the deliverable |
| Informed    | Needs to be kept in the loop on the task progress rather than roped into details of every deliverable         |

## Risk and Success Plan Review Cadence

It's crucial that your account team, under the guidance of the RSM, takes the lead in establishing a regular cadence for risk and Success Plan reviews. This involves discussing, reviewing, and documenting risks and Success Plans. The information gathered from these team reviews should then be shared with your manager, who can further disseminate it to executive leadership.

The Account Team must add Success Plan and Risk reviews to their current Account Team Sync meeting agendas. This should happen weekly or bi-weekly, either alternating between the two topics or arranging the agenda to cover both. An internal cadence for Risk ensures that information and risks identified by the account teams are highlighted early to leadership and cross-functional teams. Risk actions can be incorporated into Success Plans and shared with the customer monthly and as part of quarterly Customer Business Reviews (CBRs) . This will ensure a successful implementation of the customer's vision and journey with Splunk.

Below are the Unified Risk Management and Success Plan Review cadences that everyone is recommended to follow. You must discuss risk or customer Success Plans regularly to avoid being blindsided by your account and losing renewals and other expansion/upgrade opportunities. ## Visibility for all Account Team Members

URM creates the SFDC environment to increase risk visibility for all account team members and facilitate better collaboration in alignment with UEM. This includes:

- Account level Risk view with open counts readily seen on the main page
- Awareness of existing account risks upon new risk creation (to prevent duplication)
- Status progress of identified, validated, mitigation in progress, and closed

<!-- image -->

## Create, validate, mitigate, and close Risks

URM allows all risk phases to be performed in Salesforce. For ease of use, risks can be added from multiple areas in the tool, including Accounts, Opportunities, Success Plans, and Cases.

This creates a centralized way to create and manage risks and functions as the source of truth for the account team to collaborate on risk-related activities.

<!-- image -->

## Associate Opportunities, Success Plans &amp; Cases to Risks

URM allows risks to be associated with multiple opportunities, Success Plans, and Calls to Action (CTA) cases. This feature eliminates the need to duplicate risks to show they are affiliated with items deeper than the Account level.

Increased visibility of risks allows for better collaboration across the account team to manage them.

<!-- image -->

## Alerts/notications

URM's alerts and notifications send an email notification to the account owner when a risk has been created or closed.

This feature allows for better collaboration across the account team and Splunk Village to manage risks.

## CTA Status Definitions:

- Identified : The risk has been recognized and documented but has not yet been assessed or analyzed.
- Validating: The risk is assessed and analyzed to determine its validity, impact, and likelihood.
- Mitigation in Progress: Strategies and actions are actively being implemented to reduce the impact or likelihood of the risk.
- Closed : The risk has been closed, and there will be sub-status values to indicate the type of closure as well as a comments field.

As an Account Team Member, SEs and SEMs are responsible for helping the team identify and document all types of risk, Business and Technical. Additionally, all members of the Solutions Engineering organization are empowered to identify risk. For example, a Solutions Architect or GSS team members partnering with you can also identify risk.

## Identification

While SEs may identify business risks, SEs are most likely to identify technical risks. As an SE, you are responsible for Technical Risk Identification because your unique alignment to customers gives you the best line of sight into the customer's unique situation. Risk identified early gives Splunk the best chance to resolve the issue before it escalates, and impacts the customer's ability to realize value or decreases the probability that they will renew with Splunk.

SEMs are accountable for ensuring technical risk is identified and documented.

<!-- image -->

## Validation and Mitigation

As an SE, you are also accountable and responsible for validating and orchestrating the mitigation of all the technical risks identified for your account. So, if you are the one identifying a Technical Risk, you can just submit it, or you can begin progressing it to the Validating or

Mitigating state as part of your Technical Risk Validation/Mitigation Orchestration responsibilities. If a technical risk is identified by another Account Team member, it is your job to complete the validation process and determine how to proceed.

Remember:  You have the full force of the Splunk Technical Village available to help you mitigate technical risk!

## Closure

While the SE is accountable for ensuring each Technical Risk is closed, it is the responsibility of the SEM to transition the risk to a Closed state, providing their concurrence that the risk has been addressed.

## Communication and Collaboration

Specific to risk, be prepared to share your updates and perspectives on the Technical Risks in the account during these meetings and identify where you may need help from the Account Team or the greater Splunk Village.

Leadership meetings, including RSD/SEM Syncs, Forecasting Calls and Cross-Functional Risk Reviews, require SEMs to be up-to-date on the status of the technical risks in their accounts.

## Triggers and Signals for Solutions Engineering

You have several tools available to you as an SE as you work with your customers to address risk.

As a Solution Engineer, your role as the Technical Account owner gives you a unique perspective to identify risk triggers and signals.

You work closely with your technical champions, administrators and end users, and you orchestrate the Splunk technical village to support your customer's success. Because of this proximity, you are well-positioned to notice the signals if a customer team is not using Splunk as expected or experiencing a challenge the Splunk solution isn't addressing to their satisfaction.

As you craft your Technical Adoption Plan, leveraging the Adoption Guides, you will attach the critical activities to the associated business objectives identified in your customer's Success Plan. The progress that you and the Account Team makes with the activities in support of the business objectives helps build a strong value-focused story that should be shared during the Customer Business Review.  By connecting actions with business objectives, the Splunk Account team demonstrates our commitment to the customer's outcomes discussed prior to purchase and builds strong relationships that can be leveraged to overcome risks or position the next sales opportunity.
